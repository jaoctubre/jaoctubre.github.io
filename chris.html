The effects of different ratio and particle size combination of waste limestone on AMD

2023-06-17

For this analysis, we will use mixed models to analyze the effect of ratio and particle size in treating acid mine drainage using repeated measures anova to determine differences between treatments. We shall also use anova to determine if any of the treatment combinations have an effect on the iron content of the AMD.

Linear Mixed Model for pH Level

lmMod <- lmer(ph_level ~ time + size * ratio + (1|id), data=ph_data)
visualize(lmMod, plot="model", sample=5)

Here we have a model that takes the variables time, size, and ratio as explanatory variables with ph level as outcome. The grouping variable is size which takes into account that treatments in the same groups may be correlated to each other. This takes into consideration that there may be varying effects for each group and allows then to have their own intercepts.

*Note that the particle size does not apply to the pure lime treatment. Data is specified this way to be able to produce comparisons between the control group and the treatment groups on each level of the size and ratio variables.

To compare the different treatment levels to each other, I have combine the size and ratio variables into one variable called ‘sr’. For this, we have data and model below:

To interpret this result, let’s look at the values of the fixed effects estimates to see how each levels of ratio and particle size affect the pH level. We first need to understand that the model uses 0.075mm size and 0:100 ratio as the reference levels. With that in mind, let’s interpret the results.

Intercerpt:

The intercept is the value of the ph level when set at the reference level of 0.075mm and 0:100 ratio. So we say that the pH level is equal to 6.648 at 0.075mm and 0:100.

Time:

We say that a 1 unit increase in time increases the pH Level by 0.003709. So the pH level at 0.075mm and 0:100 ratio at 10 seconds is equal to 6.648 + (10 * 0.003819) = 6.6861

Main effects:

To interpret the main effects, we add the estimate of the ratio and size combination to the intercept. For example, if we want to estimate the ph level at 0.5mm size and 75:25 ratio and ignoring time, we have 6.648 + (-1.166) = 5.482

In summary, we just add the the estimate to the intercept to get the estimated pH level.

For multiple comparison, our null hypothesis is that there is no difference between the treatments. So if we get a p-value lower than 0.05, we must reject the null hypothesis and say that there is a difference between the means of the treatments.

Let’s look at the comparisons that are significant (those that have ’*’ on the side). Let’s take for example the comparison between 0.5&25:75 - 0.5&100:0, the multiple comparison test shows that the pH level means of these two treatment group are different from each other. We can also see that the estimate is positive which says that the mean of 0.5&25:75 treatment is higher than the mean of 0.5&100:0 treatment which suggests that the former treatment produced higher levels of pH.

The other results can be interpreted that way.
